> What makes a kanban
<!-- UID: aaa291f1-2bbe-40ac-bb23-7c751b0f0ee8 -->

> Kanban is a popular project management methodology that originated from lean manufacturing principles. It was first developed by Toyota in the 1940s to improve manufacturing efficiency. In the context of project management, Kanban provides a visual system for managing work as it moves through different stages of completion. The key components that make up a Kanban system include:
> 
> 1. Visual Board: The heart of Kanban is a visual board, often called a Kanban board, which is typically divided into columns representing different stages of the workflow. Each column represents a specific work status, such as "To Do," "In Progress," and "Done." Tasks or work items are represented as cards or sticky notes, and they move across the board as they progress through the workflow.
> 
> 2. Work-in-Progress (WIP) Limits: Kanban emphasizes limiting the number of work items that can be in progress at any given time. This helps prevent overloading the team and maintains a smooth flow of work through the system. WIP limits are set for each column on the Kanban board.
> 
> 3. Continuous Flow: Kanban encourages a continuous flow of work, with new tasks being pulled into the system only when there is capacity to work on them. This approach promotes a steady and predictable pace of work, avoiding bottlenecks and excessive multitasking.
> 
> 4. Pull System: The Kanban system operates on a "pull" basis, meaning that team members pull new work into their queue when they have the capacity to take on additional tasks. This differs from traditional "push" systems where work is assigned to individuals without considering their current workload.
> 
> 5. Feedback Loops: Kanban promotes frequent reviews and improvements through feedback loops. Teams regularly meet to discuss the flow of work, identify bottlenecks, and make adjustments to optimize their process.
> 
> 6. Lead Time and Cycle Time: Lead time is the total time it takes for a task to move from the "To Do" column to the "Done" column. Cycle time is the time it takes to complete a single task. Both metrics are used to measure the efficiency of the Kanban system and identify areas for improvement.
> 
> 7. Agile Principles: Kanban is often used in conjunction with Agile methodologies, and it aligns with Agile principles such as customer collaboration, responding to change, and delivering incremental value.
> 
> Overall, Kanban is a flexible and adaptable approach that can be applied to various industries and types of projects. It provides teams with a clear visual representation of their workflow, helps them manage their work more effectively, and facilitates continuous improvement.

> How do I set up a kanban
<!-- UID: aaa2aefb-1489-47ee-96e3-73f7ef597cef -->




>[!nexus_agent] **Assistant** - 07/22/2023 at 6:51 AM
> Setting up a Kanban system is a straightforward process. Here are the steps to get started:
> 
> 1. **Understand Your Workflow:** Begin by mapping out your existing workflow or the process through which work items pass from start to finish. Identify the different stages or steps involved, such as "To Do," "In Progress," and "Done." This step is crucial as it forms the foundation of your Kanban board.
> 
> 2. **Choose a Visualization Tool:** Decide how you want to represent your Kanban board. You can use physical tools like a whiteboard and sticky notes or a magnetic board. Alternatively, there are various digital Kanban tools available that offer features like real-time collaboration and integration with other project management systems.
> 
> 3. **Create Columns:** Set up columns on your Kanban board to reflect the stages of your workflow. For instance, you might have columns like "Backlog," "Ready," "In Progress," and "Done." The number of columns will depend on the complexity of your process.
> 
> 4. **Define Work-in-Progress (WIP) Limits:** Determine the maximum number of tasks or work items that can be in progress simultaneously for each column. WIP limits help prevent overloading team members and promote a smooth flow of work.
> 
> 5. **Visualize Tasks:** Represent individual tasks or work items as cards or sticky notes. Each task should be written clearly on a card, and it should move across the board as it progresses through the workflow.
> 
> 6. **Initiate the Kanban System:** Populate your Kanban board with the tasks that are currently in progress or in the backlog. Make sure you adhere to the WIP limits you've set.
> 
> 7. **Implement the Pull System:** As team members complete their work on a task, they can pull the next task from the "To Do" column into their "In Progress" column. This ensures that work is pulled into the system only when there's capacity to handle it.
> 
> 8. **Monitor and Review:** Regularly hold team meetings to review the Kanban board, discuss the flow of work, and identify any bottlenecks or issues. Use the feedback to make improvements and optimize the process continuously.
> 
> 9. **Measure and Analyze:** Keep track of lead time, cycle time, and other relevant metrics to gauge the efficiency of your Kanban system and identify areas for improvement.
> 
> 10. **Adapt and Evolve:** Be open to making adjustments to your Kanban system as you learn from experience. Kanban is a flexible approach, and continuous improvement is an integral part of the methodology.
> 
> Remember that implementing Kanban is not a one-time task; it's an ongoing process that involves continuous refinement to suit the specific needs of your team and organization. Regularly collaborate with your team to ensure the system remains effective and aligned with your project's goals.
